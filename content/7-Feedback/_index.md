### Overall Evaluation

**1. Working Environment**
The working environment at FCAJ is friendly and low-pressure, which made it easier to ask questions without feeling embarrassed. Most communication happens through Slack, and responses are usually quick even outside standard working hours. The only downside is that most of the internship was remote, so I didn't get to meet most members in person aside from the two community day events.

**2. Support from Mentor / Team Admin**
My mentor set clear weekly checkpoints and reviewed my worklogs regularly, which kept me accountable without feeling micromanaged. When I got stuck on a lab (especially the EC2 troubleshooting task in Week 4), the mentor gave hints rather than the direct answer, which forced me to actually debug the issue myself. The admin team was responsive whenever I had questions about the internship timeline or report format.

**3. Relevance of Work to Academic Major**
Since I study Information Technology, the networking (VPC, Transit Gateway) and database (RDS) modules connected well with what I learned in school, though the depth of hands-on AWS practice went further than any university course. Topics like CI/CD and container orchestration with ECS were largely new to me and required extra self-study outside the assigned materials.

**4. Learning & Skill Development Opportunities**
Beyond AWS-specific skills, I improved at writing clear technical documentation through the weekly worklog format, and got more comfortable using the command line for tasks I used to do only through a GUI. Attending the two community events also gave me some exposure to how AI tools are being applied in real products, which was outside the core AWS curriculum but useful context.

**5. Company Culture & Team Spirit**
Since FCAJ operates as a study-group style internship program rather than a traditional company, the "culture" here is closer to a peer-learning community — interns share notes, help each other debug labs in the group chat, and there isn't much hierarchy between cohorts. It felt collaborative rather than competitive.

**6. Internship Policies / Benefits**
The program itself is unpaid, structured around self-paced weekly labs with a fixed submission deadline for worklogs. The main benefit is the structured curriculum and access to community events like Event 1 and Event 2, which wouldn't be easy to find as a self-learner.

### Additional Questions
- **What did you find most satisfying during your internship?** Getting the EC2 remote connection issue fixed in Week 4 after a few hours of frustration — it was a small thing, but it was the first time I really felt like I understood what I was debugging instead of just following steps.
- **What do you think the company should improve for future interns?** More structured 1-on-1 mentor check-ins earlier in the program, rather than mostly async feedback through worklogs. It would also help to have a short live Q&A session after each week's material instead of purely written reference docs.
- **If recommending to a friend, would you suggest they intern here? Why or why not?** Yes, especially for someone who wants a broad, structured introduction to AWS without needing to piece together scattered tutorials themselves. It's less suited for someone looking for a fully paid, traditional corporate internship experience.

---

### Suggestions & Expectations
- **Do you have any suggestions to improve the internship experience?** A short recap or checkpoint quiz at the end of each week's material would help reinforce concepts before moving to the next topic.
- **Would you like to continue this program in the future?** I'd be interested in a follow-up track that goes deeper into one specific area, such as AWS security or DevOps, after finishing this general track.
- **Any other comments (free sharing):** Overall this was a solid foundation-building experience. The pacing was intense some weeks (especially Week 5 with Docker and ECS together), but manageable overall.
